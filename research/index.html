I am currently a project officer with the Linguistics and Multilingual Studies program at the Nanyang Technological University in Singapore.

My research interests lie primarily in language variation, identity construction, and multilingualism. Specifically, I am interested in Singapore's migrant communities and youth subcultures, and utilize both quantitative and qualitative methods in my work.

For my Honors Thesis, I investigated the rhythm of Singapore English, Mandarin, and English-Mandarin code-switching and how it varies according to social class.

Ongoing Projects

Rhythmic variation in Singapore English, Mandarin, and English-Mandarin code-switching
Honors thesis supervised by A/P Rebecca Starr at the National University of Singapore. This study examines rhythmic variation in Singapore English, Mandarin, and English-Mandarin code-switching and how it patterns according to social variables. The findings showed that the rank of a speaker's secondary school affects rhythm for Mandarin and standard English-Mandarin code-switches, as well as the extent to which a speaker maintained differences between their different codes while code-switching. Manuscript is currently in preparation.

Linguistic practices of mainland Chinese scholars in Singapore
Research project with Dr Luke Lu from the Nanyang Technological University. This project aims to examine the language practices of mainland Chinese scholars who are long-term residents in Singapore (i.e., have stayed here for more than five years) and arrived on SM1 government scholarships. Naturalistic and interview data from informants in English, Mandarin, Teochew, and Cantonese will be analyzed using methods from interactional sociolinguistics and language variation. Data collection in progress.
